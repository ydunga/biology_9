Animal tissue

Just like with plant tissues there is a wide diversity of tissues in animals.

Here we will be covering some of the common types of tissues such as epithelial tissue, animal tissues , muscular tissue and nervous tissue.

Epithelial tissue

Epithelial tissue covers most organs and cavities in animals.

It also forms a barrier to keep different body systems seperate from each other.

In terms of structure, epithelial tissue cells are tightly packed and form a continous layer. The cells have very little intercellular spaces between them.

Since the epithelium covers our organs, the permeability of these cells plays an important role in controlling the exchange of materials between our body and the external environment.

The epithelial tissue present in our intestines is different from the epithelial tissue present in our blood vessels or skin.

In our blood vessels, where transportation of substances occurs through a selectively permeable surface, the epithelial tissue is a simple flat tissue called the simple squamous epithelium.

Our skin is also made of squamous epithelium, which are arranged overlapping layers so as to prevent wear and tear. Such an arrangement of the epithelium is called stratified squamous epithelium.

In contrast to our skin tissue, there are tissues where substances are either absorbed or secreted- such as the inner lining of our intestines.

In these tissues the epithelial cells are tall and elongated. This type of tissue is known as columnar epithelium.

There also exists cuboidal epithelium which forms the lining of kidney tubuels and the ducts of salivary glands.

Connective tissue

Connective tissue is a kind of tissue that not only connects, but supports and seperates all other types of tissues in the body.

Blood is actually a kind of connective tissue!

Connective tissues have three main components:

1. Grounded substance
2. Fibers
3. Cells

The ground substance is usually a clear viscous liquid that fills the space between the cells and the fibres.

In blood cells, a fluid called plasma is present, in which red blood corpuscles (RBC's), white blood corpuscles (WBC's) and platelets are suspended.

Our bones are also another kind of connective tissue.

Bone cells are embedded in embdedded in a hard matrix which is composed of calcium and phosphorus.

Bones are connected to each other through another kind of connective tissue called ligament.

Ligament tissues are very elastic.

To connect muscle tissue and bones, there is another specialised tissue called tendons. Tendons also have great strength, but limited flexibility.

Then we have another kind of connective tissue called catrilage. Cartilage is important because smooths the bone surface at the joints.

Muscular tissue

Muscular tissue is responsible for movement in our body. These cells contain special protiens which contract and relax and cause movement!

Some muscles can be moved by us voluntarily. These muscles are called skelatal muscles as they are attached to our bones and help in body movement.

The cells of skelatal tissue are long, cylindrical and have multiple nuclei.

There are other muscles in our body whose movement we do not directly control.

For example, the movement of food in the alimentary canal is essential movement, but we do not control it.

Smooth muscles or involuntary muscles control such movements.

The movement of our heart - the rhythmic contraction and relaxation is controlled by cardiac muscles.

Nervous tissue

Cells in nervous tissues are highly specialised so that they can transmit stimulus to different parts of the body very rapidly.

Such cells are called neurons and they have a very unique structure shown below.

There are three basic types of neurons - the sensory neurons, the motor neurons and the association neurons.

Impulses passing along neurons are actual waves of electrical activity!

A neuron consists of a cell body with a nucleus and cytoplasm, from which long and thin hair like structures arise.

The long part of the neuron is called the axon and the short branched parts are called dendrites.

Many nerve fibres bound together by connective tissue make up a nerve.

The collection of all the different nerves in our body makes up our nervous system.
