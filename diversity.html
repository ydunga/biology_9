Diversity in living organisms

The natural world has a mind boggling diversity of organisms. In order to make sense of this variety, we group organisms according to observed similarities and differences.

You have already come across several characteristics based on which we can classify organisms. Some of these include mode of nutrition, mode of breathing, mode of reproduction or even cell structure (eukaryotes vs prokaryotes).

Some of the earliest classifications of organisms were proposed by biologists - Ernst Haeckel, Robert Whittaker and Carl Woese.

According to the Whittaker classification, all organisms can be divided into five kingdoms.

What is the basis of this classification you might ask?

In this classification, groups are formed on the basis of their cell structure, source of nutrition and body organisation.

In modern biology , subgroups of these kingdoms have been classified.

So, the basic unit of classification is the species. All organisms within a species are similar enough to breed amongst themselves.

We will go through the important characteristics of the five kingdoms in the following sections.

While going through the first three kingdoms, you should keep in mind the characteristics used to differentiate between them.

Monera

Organisms belonging to the Monera kingdom do not have a defined nucleus or organelles.

They also do not have multi-cellular body designs.

The mode of nutrition in these organisms can be autotrophic - synthesizing their own food, or heterotrophic - getting it from the environment.

Examples of organisms in this group include - bacteria, mycoplasma and blue green algae.

Protista

Organisms belonging to the protista kingdom are mostly unicellular. However, unlike organisms in the Monera kingdom they are eukaryotic i.e they have membrane bound organelles.

Their mode of nutrition can be autotrophic or heterotrophic.

Fungi

Mushrooms are a type of multicellular fungi, while yeast is an example of unicellular fungi.

Fungi cells are eukaryotic organisms. Unlike plant cells, which have a cell wall made of cellulose, the cell wall in fungi is made of chitin (glucose).

Fungi cannot perform photosynthesis and have to rely on external sources for nutrition.

Some fungi release enzymes which digest organic matter externally before it is absorbed by the cell.

Some other fungi require the protoplasm of a host organism to survive. Such fungi are called parasites.

There also exist certain fungal species which live in permanent mutually dependent relationships (symbiotic) with blue green algae.
