Cells - the building block of life

Just like atoms are the building blocks of all matter. Cells are the building blocks of all living matter!

You know by now that there exist unicellular and multicellular organisms. Every multicellular cells comes from a single cell which has divided into further cells.

Structure of a cell


The inner structure of a cell is immensely complex. Each component performs a specialised function, necessary for the cell.

Lets have a look at some of the main components of a cell and their functions.



First up we have the plasma membrane or the cell membrane.



The plasma membrane is the outermost covering of the cell that seperates the inner contents of the cell from the external environment.

The membrane is made of organic molecules called lipids and protiens.

The plasma membrane is selectively permeable, i.e it only allows entry and exit of some materials.

Gases like CO 2 and oxygen move across the membrane through diffusion.

Diffusion is the process of movement of a substance travelling down its concentration gradient- meaning movement from a high concerntration area to a low concentration area.

Water also moves across the membrane, in a process known as osmosis.

The fluid content inside the plasma membrane is known as cytoplasm and it contains specialised cell organelles.



Next up, we have the nucleus of the cell.



The nucleus is the control center of the cell.

The nucleus contains thread like structures called chromosomes, which contain information for the inheritance of genes from parents to their progeny in the form of DNA.

Chromosomes become visible in cells which are about to divide. In a cell which is not dividing DNA is present as chromatin.

DNA- Deoxyribo Nucleic Acid contains information necessary for constructing and organising cells.

In organisms like bacteria, the nuclear region is not clearly defined as the nucleus does not have a nuclear membrane. Such organisms are called prokaryotes. Whereas organisms with nuclear membranes are called eukaryotes.


Along with the nucleus, cell organelles help support essential chemical activities within the cell.

We'll be covering some of the major cell organelles below:

Endoplasmic reticulum



The endomplasmic reticulum is alarge network of membrane bound tubes and sheets.

There are two kinds of endoplasmic reticulum- rough and smooth.

Rough endoplasmic reticulum has particles called ribosomes attached to it. Ribosomes are the sites of protien manufacturing in the cell.

The smooth endoplasmic reticulum on the other hand helps in manufacturing fat molecules or lipids which help in building the cell membrane.

The ER system always forms a network system. This structure serves to channel different materials between the cytoplasm and the nucleus.

In the liver cells of vertebrates, the smooth ER plays a crucial role in detoxifying many posions and drugs.


The second organelle you should know about is the Golgi apparatus.



The golgi apparatus is a membrane bound organelle that is made of a series of flattened and stacked pouches called cisterns.

The golgi apparatus often has connections with the membrane of the endoplasmic reticulum.

Metrials synthesized near thr ER are packaged and transported to targets inside and outside the cells through the golgi apparatus.

The golgi apparatus is also involved in the formation of lysosomes.


Lysosomes are membrane bound sacs filled with digestive enzymes, which help to keep the cell clean by digesting any foreign material as well as worne out organelles.

The digestive enzymes within lysosomes are extremely powerful can even digest the cell itself.


Another important cell organelle that you should know about is the mitochondria.


Mitochondria are known as the power house of the cell. This is because mitochondria release ATP - Adenosine triphosphate, which is used as energy during various chemical processes.

Mitochondria actually have their own DNA and ribosomes and actually make their own protiens.



Finally, two more organelles that you should know about are plastids and vacuoles.

Plastids are organelles present only in plant cells. There are two kinds of plastids - chromoplasts and leucoplasts.

Chromoplasts contain chlorophyll, which is essential for photosynthesis.

Leucoplasts on the other hand are used for storage of starch, oils and proteins.

Plastids too, have their own DNA and ribosomes.


Vacuoles are basically like storage sacs. Many important chemical substances like amino acids and sugars are stored in vacuoles.

Animal cells have smaller vacuoles, while plant cells have very large vacuoles. They can even occupy upto 50-90% of the cell volume.

When water moves into a plant cell, the vacuole in the cell gets bigger and pushes the cell membrane against the cell wall. This increases the turgidity(bloated or swollen) of the plant cell.

Division of cells

Cells die, they grow old or get injured. New cells are formed to replace these old cells.

There are two main types of cell division through which new cells are formed - mitosis and meiosis.

Most cells in your body divide through mitosis. However gamete cells divide through meiosis!

During mitosis division, the mother cell divides into two indentical cells. The new cells will have the same number of chromosomes as the mother cell.


On the other hand, when a gamete cell divides, it produces four new cells, not two. Each of these four new cells will have only half the number of chromosomes as the mother cell.


Cells that contain two complete sets of chromosomes are called diplod cells. While cells that contain only a single set of chrmosomes are called haploids.

In technical terms, you could say that meiosis is the process of division where one diploid cell produces four haploid gametes.

During the process of fertilisation, the haploid nucleus of a sperm cell will fuse with the haploid nucleus of the egg cell and form a diploid zygote!
