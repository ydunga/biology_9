Animalia

For classification of animals, we can start with distinguishing physical characteristics.

You could think of classifying animals into two groups - one which have a backbone and others which do not have a backbone.

In fact animals with a spinal cord are called vertebrates, while animals without a spinal cord (fish) are called invertebrates.

We will look at subgroups of vertebrates and invertebrates of the animal kingdom below.

Porifera

As the name suggests, organisms classified into to the porifera subgroup have pores/holes on their body.

These holes are part of a canal system that develops throughout their body and helps in circulating water and food.

There is very little differentiation between different body parts.

Porifera are often immobile and spend most of their life cycle attached to another substance.

Porifera organisms are usually found near water bodies.

Some examples include Spongilla and Euplectella.

Coelenterata (Cnidaria)

Organisms in this subgroup are also found in water bodies.

They have more body differentiation than organisms in the Porifera group.

Common examples of Coelenterate organisms include hydra and jellyfish.

Platyhelminthes

Animals in the platyhelminthes group are far more complex than the two previously mentioned groups.

These animals are flatworms, which show extensive cell differentiation and have bilaterally symmetrical bodies.

Flatworms are hermaphrodites i.e they have both male and female organs.

Examples of platyhelminthes include tapeworms and liver flukes.

Tapeworm is a common parasite that lives inside the human gut. In order to treat a tapeworm infection, you usually need to take oral medicine which paralyze the tapeworm so that it can be removed from your gut.

Nematoda

Nematodes are the most numerous multicellular organisms on earth. Some common examples include parasitic worms such as the roundworm or the pinworm.

The body of a nematoda animal is bilaterally symmetrical.

Nematoda animals are also described as being triploblastic.

What this means is that the body is derived from three embryonic cell layers, from which differentiated tissues can be made.

Within nematoda organisms there are tissues but no real organs.

Nematodes reproduce sexually, they have male, female and hermaphrodite forms.

Annelida

The most common example of a annelida organism would be the earthworm.

What differentiates Annelida animals from Nematodes is that they have a true body cavity, where organs can be stored. Thus there is relatively more organ differentiation in annelida animals.

Organisms which have a fluid filled cavity within which organs can be suspended are called coelmates.

Arthropoda

Arthropods also have bilaterally symmetrical bodies, which are segmented.

They have an open circulatory system, meaning that blood does not flow in well defined blood vessels, instead they have a coelomic cavity filled with blood.

Some of the common examples of arthropods are butterflies, spiders and crabs.

Mollusca

Mollusc animals do not have a lot of segmentation in their body. They also have an open circulatory system.

They do have muscular feet which can used for movement.

They also usually have a hard outer shell made of calcium carbonate.

Common examples include clams and octopuses.

Echinodermata

These animals have spikes on their body and exclusively free-living marine animals.

They are triploblastic and have a coelomic cavity.

Protochordata

All sub groups we have seen until now have been invertebrates.

Animals in the protochordata group are animals which have a notochord at some stage in their life cycle.

A notochord is a long rod like support structure which runs along the back of the animals.

The notochord provides a place for muscles in the body to attach themselves and allow for easy movement.
