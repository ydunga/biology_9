When groups of similar cells work together to perform a specific function, they are called a tissue

Plant tissues

In plants, growth occurs in specific areas only. The dividing tissue in plants is known as meristematic tissue and is only located in specific areas.

Meristems make unspecialised cells which have the potential to become any kind of specialised cell!

Depending on the area they are found in, meristematic tissues have specific names.

Meristematic tissue found at the tips of stems and roots are called apical meristem.



The apical meristem increases the length of the stem and the root.

The increase in the width of the plant is controlled by lateral meristem.

Intercalary meristem tissues are found near nodes of plants and allow for stem elongation.

Since meristemic tissue is responsible for the growth of the plant, the cells in these tissues have to divide rapidly.

Meristemic cells have thin cellulose walls and a prominent nuclei.

Permanent tissue

Cells formed from meristematic tissue take up a specific role. After this point, they lose the ability to divide and form what is called permanent tissue.

The process through which meristematic cells take up a permanent shape, size and function is called differentiation.

Within permanent tissue we have simple and complex tissues.

Simple permanent tissues

Epidermis is simple permanent tissue which forms a single layer of cells covering the entire surface of the plant body.

Epidermal cells exposed to air secrete a waxy, water resistant layer on their outer surface. This helps plants protect against water loss.

In the epidermal layer we can see small pores called stomata. Stomata are necessary for exchange of gases with the atmosphere.

Structurally, stomata look like they are enclosed by two kideny shaped cells called guard cells.

Epidermal cells present on the root of the plant, have long hair like structure which increase the absorptive surface area of the plant.

Beneath the epidermis usually, you have layers of simple permanent tissue.

Parenchyma is the most common simple permanent tissue. It consists of relatively unspecialised cells. They are fairly loosely arranged, with large intercellular spaces between them.

Parenchyma tissue stores food and in some plants contain chlorophyll.

When it does contain chlorophyll, it is called chlorenchyma.

Collenchyma is another simple permanent tissue. Collenchyma allows the bending of plant parts without breaking.

Collenchyma cells are living cells, with an elongated structure. There is little intercellular space betweem them.

Sclerenchyma is another permanent tissue, which makes parts of the plant hard and stiff.

The cells of this tissue are actually dead! This tissue is often present in stems, in the veins of leaves and in the hard coverings of nuts and seeds.


Complex permanent tissue

Complex permanent tissues are tissues made of more than one type of cell.

Examples of complex permanent tissues are xylem and phloem.

Xylem helps in transporting water and mineral salts from the root of the plant to other parts of the plant.

Mature xylem cells consist of elongated dead cells, which are arranged end to end to form continous tubes.

Mature xylem cells contain no cytoplasm and have tough walls containing a woody structure called lignin.

Phloem is a complex tissue, which is used to transport amino acids and sucrose between leaves and other parts of the plant.

Unlike xylem, phloem consists of living cells arranged end to end.

Phloem is actually made up of five different types of cells - sieve cells, sieve tubes, companion cells , ploem fibres and the phloem parenchyma.
