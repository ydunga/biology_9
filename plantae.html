Plantae

Organisms classified into the plantae kingdom are multicellular eurokaryotes.

All organisms in this kingdom have cellulose cell walls. Most of them have chlorophyll, which allows them to perform photosynthesis and produce food for themselves.

Within the plantae kingdom we can differentiate between plants based on characteristics like body structure , method of reproduction and method of transportation of food and water.

Thallophyta

For instance, plants such as algae which do not have a well differentiated body are classified in the Thollophyta subgroup.

Most of these organisms tend to be aquatic.

Bryophyta

Plants in the bryophyta subgroup lack anatomical differentiation between leaves and roots.

There is no specialised tissue in the plant for conduction of water and minerals from one part of the plant to another.

Pteridophyta

Plants in this group have anatomically differentiated roots, stems and leaves.

They also have specialised tissue to transport water from one part of the plant to another.

In Thallophyta, Bryophyta and Pteridophyta, the reproductive organs are very inconspicuous i.e they are not clearly visible to the naked eye. Most of the plants in these groups reproduce through spores.

Plants which have clearly differentiated reproductive organs and reproduce by forming seeds are called phanerogams.

Within phaneorgams we can differentiate between plants based on whether their seeds are enclosed in fruits or are naked.

Gymnosperms

Gymnosperm plants bear naked seeds. Often the seeds will be enclosed in cones.

Common examples of gymnosperm plants include pines and cycads.

Angiosperms

Angiosperm plants are also called flowering plants.

Their seeds develop inside an ovary, which we know as a fruit.

Within seeds, they have structrures called cotyledons.

Cotyledons emrge and become green when the seed germinates.

Plants with seeds having a single cotyledon are called monocots, while those with two cotyledons are called dicots.
